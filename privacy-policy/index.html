Privacy Policy

Fix My Speaker is a static utility website. The core speaker cleaning tool runs in the browser and does not require an account, login, payment, or upload. The site is designed so visitors can use the tool and read guidance without submitting personal device data.

If external services are used in the future for analytics, ads, donations, or embedded media, this policy should be updated before publication. File-level privacy language is intentionally clear so visitors understand that the speaker tone itself is generated locally by browser audio functionality.

Editorial review focuses on factual clarity, safe instructions, no exaggerated recovery claims, and clear separation between tone-based cleanup and professional repair. Internal links connect trust pages with the main guides so visitors can verify who is behind the content and how to contact the site.

The site uses consistent author information, organization details, privacy language, disclaimers, and crawlable static pages so search engines and users can understand its purpose.

Readers should treat every speaker recovery guide as a controlled troubleshooting checklist. The safest process is to start with the least invasive action, listen to the result, and stop before repeating a step that is not helping. This keeps the site useful for real visitors and avoids risky advice that could make a temporary sound issue worse.

Editorially, every page is written to separate water removal, drying, cleaning, safety, and repair intent. That separation matters because a visitor searching for a quick speaker eject tool needs different guidance than a visitor dealing with salt water, repeated distortion, or a quiet earpiece after several hours.

The site also uses internal links, breadcrumbs, author signals, policy pages, and clear contact language so visitors can understand the source of the information. Those trust elements are part of the page content rather than hidden recommendations, and they can be crawled directly from the static files.

No page should encourage heat, sharp objects, compressed air pushed into the mesh, or endless loud playback. When the symptoms suggest hardware damage, the guidance points the visitor toward drying time or repair assessment instead of another unnecessary tone session.
